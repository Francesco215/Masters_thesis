The paradox of infinite fermions

When we have an infinite number of fermions a paradox can occur when you exchange a infinite number of couples

The paradox

The wavefunction of a two fermions obeys this property: $$ \psi (x_1,x_2)=-\psi (x_2,x_1) $$ Lets say that we have a system of $2n$ fermions, than it follows that on the exchange of every couple of fermions $x_1 \leftrightarrow x_2$, $x_3 \leftrightarrow x_4,\dots,x_{2n-1} \leftrightarrow x_{2n}$ we have that $$ \psi (x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4,\dots,x_{2n-1},x_{2n})=(-1)^n\psi(x_2,x_1,x_4,x_3\dots ,x_{2n},x_{2n-1}) $$ This runs into a problem when we try to do this with a (numerably) infinite number of fermions.

$$ \psi(x_1,x_2,\dots)=(-1)^\infty\psi(x_2,x_1,\dots) $$ Bosons don't have this probem because under exchange they don't change sign

Some remarks

For an uncountable number of fermions you can just get a coutable subset of fermions and the paradox will remain.

In the case of quantum field theory the paradox is written in terms of creation and annihilation operators.
